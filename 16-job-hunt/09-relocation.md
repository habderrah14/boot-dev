# Chapter 09 — Relocation

> A remote-first job search is mostly a better resume. A relocation-based one is mostly paperwork, timing, and cash — with programming as the enabling skill, not the hard part.

## Learning objectives

- Decide honestly whether relocation makes sense for your career and life.
- Map common visa paths for software engineers across major markets.
- Plan a relocation pipeline that matches visa realities (sponsorship is a filter, not a footnote).
- Budget for the move and its aftermath.

## Concept deep-dive

### Remote vs relocation vs local

Three postures, very different playbooks:

1. **Local.** Easiest paperwork, narrowest market. Fine if you live somewhere with a healthy scene (Amsterdam, Berlin, London, SF Bay, NYC, Toronto, Tel Aviv, Bangalore, Singapore).
2. **Remote.** Competitive, but flattens geography. Tax/contractor complications. Juniors face extra friction (remote-first companies skew to experienced hires).
3. **Relocation with sponsorship.** Opens specific high-paying markets. Long timelines, paperwork, and a different interview strategy.

A hybrid posture ("I'll take whichever comes") sounds pragmatic but dilutes your pipeline. Pick the primary and hedge.

### Should you relocate?

Honest prompts:

- **Career.** Will the move meaningfully improve your skills, comp, or network?
- **Life.** Do you have people/commitments where you live? What would it cost to leave?
- **Reversibility.** How hard is the move *back*, if it doesn't work?
- **Finance.** Can you afford 3–6 months of runway in the new city?
- **Family.** Partner/kids/dependents — their lives move too. Their buy-in isn't optional.

Relocate for a specific role and city, not an abstract upgrade. "Moving to SF to find a job" in a soft market is a bad bet.

### The major markets (engineer's view)

Generalities, not legal advice.

#### United States

- **H-1B.** Lottery-based specialty visa; sponsored by employer. 65k + 20k cap annually; lottery rate volatile (~25% in recent years). Tied to one employer; transfer possible.
- **O-1.** "Extraordinary ability" visa. Harder to qualify as a junior; viable for standouts with publications/open-source/awards.
- **L-1.** Intra-company transfer. If you can join a US company's foreign office first and transfer after 1 year.
- **F-1 → OPT → H-1B.** The traditional grad-school path. Slow and expensive; effective.
- **Canadian citizens (TN)**, Australians (E-3), Chileans/Singaporeans (H-1B1) have easier routes.

H-1B-sponsoring companies are public record — [myvisajobs.com](https://www.myvisajobs.com/) lists volumes by employer. Target sponsors explicitly.

#### European Union

- **EU Blue Card.** Common path for non-EU engineers with degree + salary above a threshold (varies by country; ~€45–60k typically). Path to permanent residency in a few years.
- **Germany.** Blue Card, "IT specialist" visa, "Chancenkarte" (points-based job-seeker). Mature market.
- **Netherlands.** 30% ruling (generous tax break for skilled migrants, though scaled back recently). Amsterdam/Eindhoven/Utrecht.
- **Portugal (Lisbon).** Lower salaries, lower cost of living, large tech community; tech visa pathways.
- **Ireland.** English-speaking; Critical Skills Employment Permit; strong presence of US tech companies.
- **France.** French Tech visa (Passeport Talent). Paris/Lyon/Bordeaux.
- **Spain (Barcelona, Madrid, Valencia).** Remote-worker visa introduced 2023 is useful for remote employees of foreign companies.

Generally easier and more predictable than the US lottery. Longer upskilling timelines.

#### United Kingdom

- **Skilled Worker visa.** Sponsor license required; standard path post-Brexit. £38,700 salary threshold as of 2024 (revisits itself every few years).
- **Global Talent visa.** High-skilled route, similar to US O-1 in spirit.

London dominates; Manchester/Edinburgh/Cambridge have real scenes.

#### Canada

- **Express Entry.** Points-based permanent residency. Engineers score well.
- **Global Talent Stream.** Fast work permit (often 2 weeks) sponsored by employer.
- **Permanent residency first** is a valid strategy — approved candidates can take any job.

Toronto, Vancouver, Montreal.

#### Australia / New Zealand

- **Skilled migration lists** with point-based visas; ICT occupations usually covered.
- Sydney, Melbourne, Auckland.

#### Singapore, Japan, UAE

- **Singapore Employment Pass.** Salary threshold applies; competitive market.
- **Japan HSP** (Highly Skilled Professional) points system; generous for qualifying candidates.
- **UAE Golden Visa** and ordinary work visa paths; Dubai scene growing, but cost of living bites.

### Sponsorship is a filter

The single most useful pre-application filter when relocating:

> **Does this company sponsor for candidates of your nationality at your level, right now?**

Signals:

- Explicit on the posting ("we sponsor visas").
- Past track record (public filings, LinkedIn of existing international employees, [H1BGrader](https://www.h1bgrader.com/) etc.).
- Size and maturity — large, established companies sponsor; tiny seed-stage startups rarely do.

Apply aggressively to known sponsors; deprioritize "probably won't sponsor" regardless of how good the role sounds.

### The pipeline math is different

Relocation pipelines:

- Run 2–6 months longer due to visa logistics.
- Have a lower offer rate for juniors (sponsors prefer mid+).
- Pay more — but often cost 30–50% more to live, depending on destination.

Typical timeline once you have an offer:

- **US H-1B.** Lottery in March, start in October. 7 months.
- **EU Blue Card.** 6–12 weeks from offer to work permit in most countries.
- **UK Skilled Worker.** 3–8 weeks after Certificate of Sponsorship.
- **Canada GTS.** 2–4 weeks.

Plan cash and housing around these windows. Some offers include relocation allowances — ask.

### The cost of moving

Ballpark for a mid-distance international move (one person, minimal stuff):

- **Visa fees + legal.** $500–$5,000 depending on country.
- **Flights.** $500–$2,000.
- **Temporary housing.** 4–8 weeks of short-term lets: $2,000–$6,000.
- **Deposit + first month** on a real lease: $2,000–$8,000.
- **Replacement stuff** you left behind: $1,000–$5,000.
- **Buffer for 2–3 months of lower productivity** on the new job: 0–$0 directly, but it's real.

Total: **$6,000–$25,000** range depending on destination and household.

Many companies offer relocation packages; some give a lump sum, some reimburse. Negotiate this line item explicitly.

### Taxes and remote work

Remote-across-borders is legally messy:

- **You're a tax resident where you physically live.** A US company paying you as a "W-2" while you live in Portugal is usually **not allowed** without an Employer of Record (EOR).
- **EOR services** (Deel, Remote, Oyster) are how many companies hire internationally. Ask if they use one.
- **Contractor (1099 / Freelance).** Cleaner legally, fewer benefits. You handle your own tax, social security, health.
- **Digital nomad visas.** Spain, Portugal, Estonia, Croatia, Barbados, others. Often 1–2 years, for remote workers of foreign employers.

Get professional tax advice before the first paycheck. Mistakes here cost real money.

### Relocating with a partner / family

- **Spouse work rights** vary by visa. Check before accepting.
- **Schooling** for kids — international schools are expensive, local schools require language acquisition.
- **Healthcare** systems differ. EU markets are mostly single-payer; US is employer-tied and confusing.
- **Community.** Your partner needs one too, or the move fails even if the job succeeds.

### The "return home" option

Plan for it:

- Keep your home-country bank account and mobile number working for a year.
- Don't ship every possession; storage is often cheaper than re-buying.
- Maintain the network at home — visit once a year if feasible.

Moves can go sideways. An exit plan isn't pessimism; it's prudence.

### Applying strategically as a relocation candidate

Tweaks to the playbook from [chapter 06](06-applying.md):

- Filter postings on **visa sponsorship**. Don't waste applications on "US only, no sponsorship" roles.
- Mention your **situation transparently** in the cover note or first recruiter call: "I'm currently in [country], I'd need visa sponsorship, I have [timeline flexibility]."
- Target **companies with a known sponsorship record**, weighted toward mid- and large-sized.
- Expect **extra rounds** — HR, immigration counsel calls, document collection — once you pass the technical bar.

### Remote-as-relocation-hedge

If relocation doesn't work, remote with a geo-flexible employer can be a bridge:

1. Land a remote job.
2. Move under a remote-worker or digital-nomad visa.
3. Convert to local sponsorship a year or two later, sometimes from the same employer.

The timeline is longer, but cash flow is better while you wait.

## Worked examples

### Example 1 — A junior considering Berlin

Situation: Portuguese citizen, 1 year of programming, €35k offer in Lisbon and €60k offer in Berlin.

Rough calculus:

- Lisbon: €35k, net ~€24k. Rent ~€800. Savings ~€500/mo.
- Berlin: €60k, net ~€37k. Rent ~€1,100. Savings ~€700/mo.
- Career: Berlin has a deeper engineering scene and better skill trajectory for 2–4 years.
- Life: partner needs buy-in; language learning is a multi-year project.

Many candidates would pick Berlin for 2–3 years then reassess. Both choices are defensible.

### Example 2 — A junior from Brazil eyeing the US

Situation: 2 years experience, one H-1B-sponsoring company interested, start date uncertain due to lottery.

Reality:

- H-1B lottery ~25% odds. Not winning isn't failure.
- Alternatives: L-1 via Brazil office, O-1 if portfolio strong enough, Canada as a stepping stone.
- Realistic 3-year plan: apply lottery Year 1 (lose), take Canada Express Entry role Year 1, re-apply lottery Year 2 (win), move to US Year 3. Works, but patience is required.

Plan for the likely path, not the lucky one.

## Common pitfalls & gotchas

- **Applying to non-sponsors while needing sponsorship.** Silent funnel leak.
- **Ignoring taxes.** A surprise tax bill post-move is a genuinely bad time.
- **Relocating for the abstract** ("SF is the best place to be an engineer") without a concrete role and city reality.
- **Underestimating cash needs.** The first 3 months post-move cost more than you expect.
- **Assuming remote = no borders.** Tax residency and work authorization still apply.
- **Not asking about relocation allowance.** Many companies offer $5–25k if asked.
- **Skipping legal advice.** Read the contract, visa conditions, and tax guidance before signing.

## Exercises

1. **Warm-up.** Pick 1–2 target countries. List 10 companies with a demonstrated sponsorship record for your nationality.
2. **Standard.** Research the exact visa(s) you'd need. Write a 1-paragraph summary of each: eligibility, timeline, renewal, path to permanent residency.
3. **Bug hunt.** A friend has applied to 50 US roles needing H-1B sponsorship; 35 were early-stage startups. Diagnose the top funnel leak.
4. **Stretch.** Build a relocation budget spreadsheet with the six cost categories above, using real quotes from rental/airline/visa sites for your target city.
5. **Stretch++.** Talk to one engineer already working in your target city (via [networking](07-networking.md) practice). Ask how their move actually went — not the polished LinkedIn version.
